World Domination Strategy Game

History

Risk was invented in 1957 by French film director Albert Lamorisse and originally released in France as La Conqu√™te du Monde (The Conquest of the World). The game was inspired by Lamorisse's love of strategy and world geography.

In 1959, Parker Brothers bought the rights and released it in the United States as Risk: The Continental Game, later renamed Risk: The Game of Global Domination. The game became one of the most popular board games in history, inspiring other strategy games like Axis & Allies and Settlers of Catan.

Evolution of Risk

  • 1957: Original French release as La Conqu√™te du Monde
  • 1959: Parker Brothers US release
  • 1986: Castle Risk - European castles theme (financial disappointment)
  • 1993: Secret Mission rules added to US edition
  • 2001: Risk: 2210 A.D. - Futuristic theme with moon and ocean territories
  • 2002+: Themed versions (Lord of the Rings, Star Wars, Transformers, etc.)
  • 2008: 1959 Risk - Reproduction of original version

Today, Risk is still in production by Hasbro with numerous editions, variants, video games, and mobile apps. The game has sold millions of copies worldwide and remains a classic strategy game.

Game Rules

Objective

The goal is to occupy every territory on the board and eliminate all other players. In some variants, players have secret missions that shorten the game.

The Board

The world map is divided into 42 territories, grouped into 6 continents:

Continent Territories Bonus Armies
North America 9 +5
South America 4 +2
Europe 7 +5
Africa 6 +3
Asia 12 +7
Australia 4 +2

Gameplay Phases

Each turn consists of three phases:

  1. Reinforcement: Receive new armies based on:
    • Number of territories controlled (minimum 3, or 1 per 3 territories)
    • Continent bonuses (if you control all territories in a continent)
    • Risk cards (trade sets of 3 cards for bonus armies)
  2. Attack: Attack adjacent enemy territories using dice:
    • Attacker rolls up to 3 dice (must leave 1 army behind)
    • Defender rolls up to 2 dice
    • Highest dice compared, loser loses armies
    • Continue until attacker stops or is defeated
  3. Fortification: Move armies from one territory to an adjacent friendly territory (once per turn)

Combat System

Combat uses dice rolls:

  • Attacker: Up to 3 dice (must have at least 2 armies to attack)
  • Defender: Up to 2 dice
  • Dice are compared highest to highest, second highest to second highest
  • Defender wins ties
  • Loser removes armies equal to attacker's wins

Risk Cards

Players receive a Risk card at the end of each turn if they conquered at least one territory. Cards show:

  • Territory name
  • Symbol: Infantry, Cavalry, or Artillery

Trade sets of 3 cards (same symbol, or one of each, or two plus wild) for bonus armies. The value increases as more sets are traded.

Winning

Win by eliminating all opponents and controlling all 42 territories. In Secret Mission variants, complete your secret objective.

Strategy & Tactics

Basic Strategy

  • Continent Control: Controlling entire continents provides bonus armies each turn. Australia (+2) and South America (+2) are easiest to hold. Asia (+7) is powerful but hard to defend.
  • Chokepoints: Control key territories that connect continents (e.g., Middle East, Egypt, Ukraine) to block enemy movement.
  • Army Placement: Concentrate armies on borders with enemies, not in safe interior territories.
  • Risk Cards: Don't trade cards too early - values increase. But don't hoard if you need armies.

Attack Strategy

  • When to Attack: Attack when you have numerical advantage (3:2 or better)
  • Blitzing: Use large armies to quickly capture multiple territories in one turn
  • Defensive Play: Sometimes it's better to fortify and wait for better odds
  • Elimination: Eliminating a player gives you all their Risk cards - powerful but risky

Defense Strategy

  • Fortification: Always fortify at end of turn - move armies to front lines
  • Chokepoints: Defend key territories that block enemy advances
  • Interior Defense: Keep minimum armies in safe interior territories
  • Alliances: Form temporary alliances (though not in this digital version)

Continent Priorities

  • Australia: Easiest to hold, good starting point (+2 bonus)
  • South America: Only 4 territories, easy to defend (+2 bonus)
  • Africa: Moderate difficulty, 3 entry points (+3 bonus)
  • North America: Strong bonus (+5) but 3 entry points
  • Europe: High bonus (+5) but many borders, hard to hold
  • Asia: Highest bonus (+7) but 12 territories and many borders - very difficult

Game Variants

Secret Mission Risk

Each player receives a secret mission card (e.g., "Conquer Asia and Africa"). Completing your mission wins the game, making it shorter than world domination.

Risk: 2210 A.D.

Futuristic version with moon territories, ocean territories, commander units, and nuclear weapons. Limited to 5 turns.

Tips for Beginners

  • Start Small: Focus on controlling Australia or South America first - they're easier to hold
  • Fortify Always: Never skip the fortification phase - move armies to borders
  • Don't Overextend: Don't spread armies too thin - concentrate on key areas
  • Watch Borders: Always check which territories border enemies
  • Risk Cards Matter: Collect and trade cards strategically
  • Patience: Sometimes waiting and building up is better than attacking
  • Learn the Map: Know which territories connect to which - geography is key

Cultural Impact

Risk has had a significant cultural impact since 1957:

  • One of the best-selling board games of all time
  • Inspired numerous strategy games and video games
  • Featured in movies and TV shows
  • Used in educational settings to teach geography and strategy
  • Has a dedicated competitive scene with tournaments
  • Multiple video game adaptations (PC, console, mobile)

The game's combination of strategy, luck, and diplomacy has made it a timeless classic that continues to be enjoyed by millions worldwide.
