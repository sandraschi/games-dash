The strategic corner-to-corner race

üìú The Rules

Objective: Move all your pieces from your starting corner to the opposite corner before your opponent.

How to Play

  • Play on a 16√ó16 board
  • Each player starts with 19 pieces in their corner
  • Pieces can move in two ways:
    • Step: Move to an adjacent empty square (8 directions)
    • Jump: Jump over a piece (any piece) to an empty square beyond
  • Jumps can be chained - make multiple jumps in one turn!
  • First player to get all pieces to the opposite corner wins

Movement Rules

You can move to any adjacent empty square, or jump over any piece (yours or opponent's) to an empty square. Chain jumps are allowed - if you can jump again after landing, you can continue jumping in the same turn.

üéØ Strategy Tips

Opening Strategy

  • Move forward: Get pieces out of the starting zone quickly
  • Create jump chains: Set up pieces to enable long jump sequences
  • Block opponent: Position pieces to slow down opponent's progress

Mid Game

  • Jump chains: Master the art of chaining jumps for maximum distance
  • Path planning: Plan routes that allow multiple jumps
  • Balance: Don't leave pieces behind - move as a group

End Game

  • Fill the corner: Get all pieces into the end zone
  • Final positioning: Arrange pieces to fill all spaces efficiently
  • Blocking: Use pieces to block opponent's final moves

üìö History

Halma was invented in 1883-1884 by George Howard Monks, a U.S. thoracic surgeon. The name comes from the Greek word "halma" meaning "jump."

Variants

Halma is the precursor to Chinese Checkers, which uses a star-shaped board. The game was popular in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Board Sizes

Halma can be played on different board sizes. The 16√ó16 board with 19 pieces per player is the standard version, but smaller variants exist for faster games.

üí° Common Mistakes

  • Ignoring jumps: Jumps are much faster than steps - use them!
  • Leaving pieces behind: Move all pieces forward together
  • Missing chain opportunities: Look for multiple jump sequences
  • Poor end zone planning: Plan how pieces will fit in the final corner
