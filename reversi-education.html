The classic flipping strategy game

üìú The Rules

Objective: Have more pieces of your color on the board when the game ends.

How to Play

  • Play on an 8√ó8 board
  • Black plays first
  • Place a piece on an empty square that flips one or more opponent pieces
  • Pieces are flipped when they're between your new piece and another of your pieces
  • You can flip pieces in any of the 8 directions (horizontal, vertical, diagonal)
  • If you can't make a valid move, your turn is skipped
  • Game ends when neither player can move
  • Player with most pieces wins!

Flipping Rules

When you place a piece, all opponent pieces in a straight line (in any direction) between your new piece and another of your pieces are flipped to your color. You must flip at least one piece to make a valid move.

üéØ Strategy Tips

Early Game

  • Avoid corners early: Corners are powerful, but playing them too early can backfire
  • Control edges: Edge squares are valuable but risky
  • Minimize flips: Sometimes it's better to flip fewer pieces to maintain control

Mid Game

  • Corner strategy: Corners are permanent - once captured, they can't be flipped
  • Edge control: Control edges to limit opponent's options
  • Mobility: Keep your options open - having many valid moves is key

End Game

  • Count pieces: When few moves remain, count to see who's winning
  • Force bad moves: Make moves that force opponent into disadvantageous positions
  • Corner capture: Corners become crucial in the endgame

üìö History

Reversi was invented in 1883 by Lewis Waterman and John W. Mollett in England. The modern version, Othello, was trademarked in 1971 by Goro Hasegawa in Japan.

Name Origins

The name "Othello" comes from Shakespeare's play, referencing the theme of black and white. The game became extremely popular in Japan and spread worldwide.

Competitive Play

Othello has a strong competitive scene with world championships held annually. The game is mathematically complex, with over 10^28 possible positions, making it impossible to solve completely.

üí° Common Mistakes

  • Giving away corners: Never play adjacent to a corner unless necessary
  • Maximizing flips: More flips isn't always better - quality over quantity
  • Ignoring mobility: Having few valid moves is dangerous
  • Edge obsession: Edges are important, but corners are king
