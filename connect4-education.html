üü° Connect Four - Learn & Strategy

The vertical tic-tac-toe game

üìú The Rules

Objective: Be the first to connect four of your pieces in a row (horizontally, vertically, or diagonally).

How to Play

  • Players take turns dropping colored discs into a 7√ó6 grid
  • Discs fall to the lowest available space in the chosen column
  • Red player goes first
  • First to get four in a row wins!

Winning Conditions

  • Horizontal: Four pieces in a row across
  • Vertical: Four pieces stacked vertically
  • Diagonal: Four pieces diagonally (both directions)

üéØ Strategy Tips

Defensive Play

  • Block threats: Always block your opponent's three-in-a-row
  • Control the center: Center columns offer more winning opportunities
  • Watch diagonals: Diagonal wins are hardest to spot - be vigilant!

Offensive Play

  • Create multiple threats: Set up two potential wins at once
  • Use the bottom: Pieces at the bottom are harder to block
  • Force moves: Make your opponent block, then exploit the opening

Advanced Tactics

  • Fork: Create two winning threats simultaneously
  • Sacrifice: Sometimes let opponent win a column to set up bigger win
  • Column control: Dominating center columns gives you more options

üìö History

Connect Four was invented in 1974 by Howard Wexler and Ned Strongin. Originally called "The Captain's Mistress," it was first published by Milton Bradley (now Hasbro).

Mathematical Solution

Connect Four was solved in 1988! With perfect play, the first player can always force a win. However, this requires perfect play from both players - in practice, games are still exciting and competitive.

Popularity

Connect Four became a classic family game and has been featured in tournaments, computer programs, and even as a game show format. It's simple to learn but offers deep strategic play.

üí° Common Mistakes

  • Ignoring opponent threats: Always check for three-in-a-row
  • Playing only offense: Defense is crucial - block first, attack second
  • Neglecting edges: Edge columns have fewer winning combinations
  • Missing diagonals: Diagonal wins are easy to overlook
