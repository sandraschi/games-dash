üìú The Rules

Objective: Sink all of your opponent's ships before they sink yours.

Setup

  • Each player has 5 ships:
    • Carrier (5 cells)
    • Battleship (4 cells)
    • Cruiser (3 cells)
    • Submarine (3 cells)
    • Destroyer (2 cells)
  • Place ships horizontally or vertically on your 10√ó10 grid
  • Ships cannot overlap or be placed diagonally
  • Ships cannot touch each other (optional rule)

Gameplay

  • Players take turns calling out coordinates (e.g., "B5")
  • Hit (‚úï): You hit an enemy ship
  • Miss (‚óã): You hit empty water
  • When all cells of a ship are hit, it's sunk
  • First to sink all enemy ships wins!

üéØ Strategy Tips

Ship Placement

  • Spread out: Don't cluster ships together
  • Mix orientations: Use both horizontal and vertical placements
  • Avoid patterns: Don't place ships in obvious rows/columns
  • Edge placement: Ships on edges are harder to find
  • Corner strategy: Corners can be safe or risky - use wisely

Search Strategy

  • Checkerboard pattern: Efficient way to search the board
  • After a hit: Search adjacent cells systematically
  • Ship hunting: Once you find a ship, determine its orientation
  • Probability: Center squares are more likely to contain ships

Advanced Tactics

  • Salvo mode: Fire multiple shots per turn (if allowed)
  • Memory: Track your shots to avoid repeating
  • Pattern recognition: Learn opponent's placement tendencies
  • Bluffing: React to hits/misses to mislead opponent

üìö History

Battleship originated as a pencil-and-paper game in the early 1900s. It was commercialized by Milton Bradley in 1967 and became a classic strategy game.

Original Name

The game was originally called "Broadsides" and was played on graph paper. Players would draw their fleets and call out coordinates to attack.

Pop Culture

Battleship has appeared in movies, TV shows, and even had a 2012 film adaptation. The game's simple rules and strategic depth have kept it popular for over a century.

üí° Common Mistakes

  • Clustering ships: Makes them easy to find once one is discovered
  • Predictable patterns: Placing ships in rows/columns is too obvious
  • Forgetting shots: Keep track of where you've already fired
  • Giving up after first hit: One hit doesn't mean the game is lost
  • Ignoring probability: Some areas are statistically more likely to contain ships
