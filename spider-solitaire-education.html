Spider Solitaire: History & Strategy

The complex solitaire challenge

History of Spider Solitaire

Origins

Spider Solitaire is a more complex variant of solitaire that uses two decks of cards. The exact origins are unclear, but it likely developed in the early 20th century as a more challenging alternative to Klondike Solitaire.

The name "Spider" may refer to the spider-like appearance of the tableau when cards are spread out, or to the way sequences "crawl" down the columns like a spider's legs.

Microsoft Spider Solitaire

Spider Solitaire became widely known when Microsoft included it in Windows 98 Plus! pack, and later in Windows XP. It quickly became a favorite among solitaire enthusiasts who found Klondike too easy or too luck-dependent.

Microsoft's version offered three difficulty levels:

  • Easy (1 Suit): All cards are the same suit - easiest to win
  • Medium (2 Suits): Two suits - moderate difficulty
  • Hard (4 Suits): All four suits - very challenging

Why It's Popular

Spider Solitaire appeals to players who want:

  • More Strategy: Less luck-dependent than Klondike
  • Longer Games: Games can take 10-30 minutes, providing more engagement
  • Complex Puzzles: Requires careful planning and sequence management
  • Higher Win Rate: With skill, you can win most games

Rules of Spider Solitaire

Objective

Build eight complete sequences (King down to Ace) in the same suit. When a complete sequence is built, it's automatically removed from the tableau.

Setup

  • Uses two standard decks (104 cards total for 4-suit version)
  • 10 tableau columns are dealt: first 4 columns get 6 cards each, last 6 columns get 5 cards each
  • Only the top card of each column is face-up
  • Remaining cards form the stock pile
  • 8 foundation areas (one for each completed sequence)

How to Play

  • Tableau Building: Build sequences in descending order (King to Ace) in the SAME suit
  • Moving Sequences: You can move sequences of cards in the same suit as a unit
  • Dealing: When no moves are possible, deal 10 cards (one to each column) from the stock
  • Revealing Cards: When you move a face-up card, the card beneath it is revealed
  • Empty Columns: Any card or sequence can be placed in an empty column
  • Complete Sequences: When a King-to-Ace sequence is built in one suit, it's automatically removed

Key Differences from Klondike

  • Same Suit Only: Sequences must be in the same suit (not alternating colors)
  • Two Decks: Uses 104 cards instead of 52
  • No Foundation Piles: Complete sequences are automatically removed
  • Deal All Columns: When dealing, all 10 columns get a new card

Start with 1 suit (Easy) to learn the game. Once comfortable, try 2 suits (Medium). 4 suits (Hard) is very challenging and requires excellent planning and sequence management.

Strategy & Tips

Basic Strategy

  • Expose Hidden Cards: Prioritize moves that reveal face-down cards
  • Build Same-Suit Sequences: Focus on building sequences in the same suit
  • Empty Columns: Create empty columns - they're essential for moving long sequences
  • Don't Deal Too Early: Only deal when you've exhausted all possible moves
  • Plan Sequence Moves: Think about how to use empty columns to move long sequences

Advanced Techniques

  • Sequence Management: Keep sequences organized by suit - don't mix suits unnecessarily
  • Empty Column Strategy: Use empty columns to temporarily store cards while reorganizing
  • Deal Timing: Deal only when absolutely necessary - each deal adds complexity
  • Suits Priority: In 4-suit games, focus on completing one suit at a time
  • Blocking Prevention: Avoid moves that block access to important cards

Common Mistakes

  • Dealing Too Quickly: Always look for moves before dealing
  • Mixing Suits: Don't build sequences with mixed suits - they can't be moved
  • Wasting Empty Columns: Don't fill empty columns with single cards - save them for sequences
  • Not Planning Ahead: Think several moves ahead, especially sequence moves
  • Ignoring Suit Organization: Keep cards organized by suit for easier sequence building

In 4-suit Spider Solitaire, try to complete sequences one suit at a time. Focus on the suit that has the most exposed cards or the best sequence-building opportunities.
