üìú History

1974

Invention: Ern≈ë Rubik, a Hungarian architecture professor, creates the first prototype of the cube as a teaching tool to help his students understand 3D geometry and spatial relationships. He initially calls it the "Magic Cube" (B≈±v√∂s kocka in Hungarian).

1975

Patent Application: Rubik files for a Hungarian patent for his "Spatial Logical Toy." The patent is granted in 1977.

1977

First Production: A small Hungarian company, Politechnika, begins manufacturing the cube. Initial production is limited to a few thousand units sold in Hungary.

1979

International Debut: The cube is showcased at the Nuremberg Toy Fair in Germany. Tom Kremer, a British toy specialist, sees the cube and recognizes its potential. He negotiates a deal to distribute it worldwide.

1980

Global Launch: Ideal Toy Company licenses the cube for worldwide distribution. The name is changed from "Magic Cube" to "Rubik's Cube" to honor its inventor. The cube launches in the United States and quickly becomes a global phenomenon.

1980-1982

The Cube Craze: The Rubik's Cube becomes the fastest-selling puzzle in history. Over 100 million cubes are sold worldwide. Books, solution guides, and competitions spring up everywhere. The cube appears on magazine covers, TV shows, and becomes a cultural icon.

1982

First World Championship: The first official Rubik's Cube World Championship is held in Budapest, Hungary. Minh Thai from the United States wins with a time of 22.95 seconds.

1980s Decline

Market Saturation: By the mid-1980s, the initial craze subsides. Millions of cubes sit unsolved in drawers worldwide. However, a dedicated community of speedcubers continues to practice and compete.

2000s Revival

Internet Era: The internet brings cubers together. Online communities, tutorials, and competitions flourish. New solving methods are developed and shared. The World Cube Association (WCA) is founded in 2004 to organize official competitions.

Present Day

Modern Era: The Rubik's Cube remains the world's best-selling puzzle. Speedcubing has become a serious sport with thousands of competitions annually. New cube designs, lubricants, and solving techniques continue to push the boundaries of what's possible.

üë§ The Inventor: Ern≈ë Rubik

Early Life

Ern≈ë Rubik was born on July 13, 1944, in Budapest, Hungary, during World War II. His father, Ern≈ë Rubik Sr., was an aircraft engineer, and his mother, Magdolna Sz√°nt√≥, was a poet. Growing up in post-war Hungary, Rubik developed a fascination with geometry, architecture, and three-dimensional objects.

Education & Career

Rubik studied architecture at the Budapest University of Technology (1962-1967) and later studied sculpture at the Academy of Applied Arts and Design. In 1971, he became a professor at the Budapest College of Applied Arts, where he taught interior design and architecture.

As a professor, Rubik was always looking for ways to help his students understand spatial relationships and three-dimensional geometry. He believed that hands-on learning was essential for understanding complex concepts.

The Moment of Inspiration

In the spring of 1974, Rubik was working on a problem: how to create a structure where individual parts could move independently without falling apart. He wanted to design a teaching tool that would help students understand how parts of a whole can move independently while remaining connected.

He began experimenting with wooden blocks, rubber bands, and paper clips. After several weeks of trial and error, he created a 3√ó3√ó3 cube with 26 smaller cubes (called "cubies") that could rotate on three axes. The first prototype was made of wood and held together with rubber bands and paper clips.

The first time Rubik scrambled his cube, it took him over a month to solve it. He had created a puzzle that even he, the inventor, found challenging!

Life After the Cube

Despite the cube's massive success, Rubik remained humble and continued his work as an educator. He used his wealth to support education and innovation in Hungary. He founded the Rubik Studio, which designs furniture and games, and continues to work on new puzzles and educational tools.

Rubik has received numerous awards, including the Kossuth Prize (Hungary's highest cultural award) and honorary doctorates from universities worldwide. He remains active in promoting education and innovation, particularly in mathematics and engineering.

Quote from Ern≈ë Rubik: "The cube is a very simple object, but it's also very complex. It's a paradox. The simplicity is in the structure, but the complexity is in the possibilities."

üß© Solving Methods

There are many methods to solve the Rubik's Cube, each with different trade-offs between speed, memorization, and ease of learning.

1. Beginner's Method (Layer-by-Layer)

Difficulty: Easy to learn
Moves: ~100-120 moves
Speed: 1-3 minutes (beginners), 30-60 seconds (practiced)

Steps:

  1. Solve the white cross (bottom layer edges)
  2. Solve the white corners (complete bottom layer)
  3. Solve the middle layer edges
  4. Solve the yellow cross (top layer)
  5. Orient the yellow edges
  6. Position the yellow corners
  7. Orient the yellow corners

Best for: First-time solvers, understanding cube mechanics

2. CFOP (Fridrich Method)

Difficulty: Advanced
Moves: ~55-60 moves
Speed: 10-20 seconds (world-class), 20-30 seconds (advanced)

Steps:

  1. Cross: Solve the bottom cross (intuitive)
  2. F2L (First Two Layers): Solve bottom and middle layers simultaneously (41 algorithms)
  3. OLL (Orient Last Layer): Orient all top layer pieces (57 algorithms)
  4. PLL (Permute Last Layer): Position all top layer pieces (21 algorithms)

Best for: Speedcubing, most popular method among competitive cubers

3. Roux Method

Difficulty: Advanced
Moves: ~48-50 moves
Speed: 8-15 seconds (world-class)

Steps:

  1. Build a 1√ó2√ó3 block on the left
  2. Build a 1√ó2√ó3 block on the right
  3. Solve the top corners and edges (CMLL - 42 algorithms)
  4. Solve the remaining 6 edges (LSE - intuitive)

Best for: Speedcubing, fewer algorithms than CFOP, more intuitive

4. ZZ Method

Difficulty: Advanced
Moves: ~50-55 moves
Speed: 10-18 seconds (world-class)

Steps:

  1. EO (Edge Orientation): Orient all edges during inspection
  2. Line: Build a line on the bottom
  3. F2L: Solve first two layers (similar to CFOP but with oriented edges)
  4. LL (Last Layer): Solve the last layer (only 7 PLL algorithms needed)

Best for: Speedcubing, efficient move count, good for one-handed solving

5. Petrus Method

Difficulty: Intermediate-Advanced
Moves: ~50-60 moves
Speed: 15-25 seconds (advanced)

Steps:

  1. Build a 2√ó2√ó2 block
  2. Expand to 2√ó2√ó3 block
  3. Orient edges (EO)
  4. Complete first two layers
  5. Solve last layer

Best for: Understanding cube structure, efficient solving

6. Corners First Methods

Difficulty: Intermediate
Moves: ~80-100 moves
Speed: 30-60 seconds

Methods like Waterman and Ortega solve corners first, then edges. Less popular today but historically significant.

Choosing a Method

  • Beginners: Start with Layer-by-Layer method
  • Speedcubing: CFOP is most popular, Roux is gaining popularity
  • Fewest Moves: Human Thistlethwaite or advanced block-building
  • One-Handed: ZZ or CFOP with modifications
  • Blindfolded: Specialized methods like Old Pochmann or 3-Style

üî¢ Mathematical Aspects

The Rubik's Cube is a fascinating mathematical object that has inspired research in group theory, combinatorics, algorithms, and computational complexity.

Group Theory

The Rubik's Cube can be mathematically described as a group - a fundamental concept in abstract algebra. Each move on the cube is an element of the Rubik's Cube group.

The Rubik's Cube Group

Definition: The Rubik's Cube group is the group of all legal positions reachable from the solved state through valid moves.

  • Group Size: 43,252,003,274,489,856,000 (approximately 4.3√ó10¬π‚Åπ) positions
  • Generators: The 6 basic face turns (U, D, F, B, R, L) and their inverses
  • Group Structure: A subgroup of the symmetric group S‚ÇÑ‚Çà (permutations of 48 facelets)
  • Subgroups: The cube has many interesting subgroups, including corner-only and edge-only groups

Permutations and Combinations

Corner Permutations

The 8 corner pieces can be arranged in 8! = 40,320 ways. However, only half of these are reachable (20,160 valid positions) due to parity constraints.

Edge Permutations

The 12 edge pieces can be arranged in 12! = 479,001,600 ways. Again, only half are reachable (239,500,800 valid positions) due to parity.

Orientation Constraints

Corner Orientations: Each corner can be oriented in 3 ways, but the sum of all corner orientations must be divisible by 3. This gives 3‚Å∑ = 2,187 valid corner orientations.

Edge Orientations: Each edge can be oriented in 2 ways, but the sum must be even. This gives 2¬π¬π = 2,048 valid edge orientations.

Total Positions Calculation

The total number of reachable positions is:

(8! √ó 3‚Å∑ √ó 12! √ó 2¬π¬π) / 12 = 43,252,003,274,489,856,000

Why divide by 12? The division accounts for:

  • Parity constraints (corner and edge permutations must have same parity) - factor of 2
  • Corner orientation constraint (sum must be divisible by 3) - factor of 3
  • Edge orientation constraint (sum must be even) - factor of 2
  • Total: 2 √ó 3 √ó 2 = 12

God's Number

God's Number is the minimum number of moves required to solve any scrambled cube position in the worst case.

God's Number = 20

In 2010, after 35 years of research and using Google's computing resources, mathematicians proved that God's Number is 20.

This means:

  • Every scrambled cube can be solved in 20 moves or fewer
  • Some positions require exactly 20 moves (called "superflip" positions)
  • The proof required checking all 43+ quintillion positions

God's Number by Metric

  • Quarter-Turn Metric (QTM): 26 moves (counting 90¬∞ turns only)
  • Half-Turn Metric (HTM): 20 moves (counting 90¬∞ and 180¬∞ turns)
  • Slice-Turn Metric (STM): 20 moves (includes slice moves)

Most cubers use HTM, which is why God's Number is commonly stated as 20.

Algorithm Complexity

Optimal Solving Algorithms

Kociemba's Algorithm: Two-phase algorithm that can find near-optimal solutions (typically 19-20 moves) in seconds.

Thistlethwaite's Algorithm: Four-phase algorithm that guarantees solutions in 52 moves or fewer (theoretical, not optimal).

IDA* (Iterative Deepening A*): Used to find optimal solutions, but computationally expensive for full solves.

Computational Complexity

Finding a Solution: The problem of finding any solution is in NP (nondeterministic polynomial time).

Finding Optimal Solution: Finding the shortest solution is NP-hard, meaning it's computationally difficult.

Practical Solving: Modern algorithms can find near-optimal solutions (19-20 moves) in seconds using heuristics and lookup tables.

Symmetry and Automorphisms

The Rubik's Cube has 24 rotational symmetries (the cube can be rotated in 24 different ways that preserve its structure).

Symmetry Operations

  • Face Rotations: 6 ways to choose the top face √ó 4 rotations = 24 symmetries
  • Automorphism Group: The group of symmetries has order 24
  • Equivalent Positions: Many positions are equivalent under rotation, reducing the effective search space

Mathematical Properties

Parity

The cube has parity constraints that prevent certain positions:

  • Corner Parity: Corner permutation parity must match edge permutation parity
  • Orientation Parity: Sum of corner orientations must be divisible by 3
  • Edge Parity: Sum of edge orientations must be even

These constraints mean only 1/12 of all possible arrangements are actually reachable.

Conjugacy Classes

Many cube positions are conjugate to each other - they have the same structure but in different locations. Understanding conjugacy helps in algorithm development and pattern recognition.

Commutators and Conjugates

Commutators: Sequences like [A, B] = A B A' B' are fundamental building blocks of cube algorithms. They allow precise control over specific pieces.

Conjugates: Sequences like A B A' allow applying algorithm B in a different context. Most speedcubing algorithms are conjugates of simpler sequences.

Mathematical Research Applications

  • Group Theory: The cube is used to teach and research group theory concepts
  • Algorithm Design: Research into optimal solving algorithms has advanced search algorithms
  • Computational Complexity: The cube is a benchmark problem for AI and optimization
  • Graph Theory: The cube's state space forms a Cayley graph with interesting properties
  • Combinatorics: Counting and enumerating cube positions is a rich area of research

Interesting Mathematical Facts

  • Superflip: A position requiring exactly 20 moves to solve, where all edges are flipped
  • Superflip + 4-Spot: Another 20-move position with flipped edges and 4 corners swapped
  • Maximum Order: The highest order of any element in the cube group is 1,260 (a position that requires 1,260 moves to return to itself)
  • Diameter: The diameter of the cube's state graph is 20 (God's Number)
  • Average Distance: The average number of moves to solve a random position is approximately 18
